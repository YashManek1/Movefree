Abstract  - MoveFree functions as a navigation assistant 
which visually impaired users can wear to receive spatial 
information throughout their journey while maintaining 
mobility and remote safety supervision. The system 
combines a custom -trained YOLOv8s outdoor model  
which detects potholes and road hazards with a 
YOLOv11m indoor model that recognizes 19 object 
classes which include furniture and stairs and doors and 
trip-hazard objects. The hardware system consists of two 
ergonomic units which include smart  glasses that contain 
the camera and bone -conduction headphones and 
microphone and vibration motor and a belt -mounted 
processing box which houses an NVIDIA Jetson Nano and 
GPS module and power supply and wireless modules. A 
companion mobile application pro vides real -time live 
video streaming of the user's point -of-view to registered 
caregivers, live GPS location sharing which updates every 
three to five seconds, and SOS emergency alert dispatch 
which uses one -tap notifications. The indoor model 
achieves an overall mAP@0.5 of 81.8% with safety -
critical performance of 95.0% mAP on stairs and 95.6% 
on persons, while the outdoor pothole model attains 
76.69% mAP@0.5 on the test set. The complete inference 
pipeline operates at approximately 32 to 40 frames per 
second on the Jetson Nano GPU, and it runs at an 
estimated 10 to 13 frames per second in prototype 
Raspberry Pi 5 deployments. MoveFree provides an 
affordable and scalable mobility solution which uses 
intelligent context -aware technology to create a system 
that combines multi -modal sensing with on -device AI 
inference and caregiver communication  into a single 
wearable device . 
Keywords: Assistive technology, Wearable devices, Edge 
computing, Object detection, YOLO, Navigation systems, 
GPS tracking, Real -time systems  
I. INTRODUCTION  The most important obstacle to people who want to 
travel independently throughout the world exists 
because of visual impairment. The World Health 
Organization's World Report on Vision (2019) 
estimates that approximately 2.2 billion people 
worldwide experie nce some degree of vision loss 
which includes at least 36 million people who are 
classified as blind. India alone accounts for over 12 
million visually impaired individuals who must 
navigate public spaces that include unpaved roads 
unmarked curbs and areas  with heavy pedestrian and 
vehicular traffic.  
People use white canes and guide dogs as their main 
mobility tools because those tools provide basic 
mobility assistance but they do not help users 
understand their spatial environment or assist them 
in finding their way or understanding their 
surroundings . The Envision Glasses and WeWALK 
Smart Cane 2.0 commercial smart assistive devices 
show some advancements over traditional devices 
but they remain too expensive for most people who 
live in developing economies while they lack the 
ability to detect hazards  through multiple methods at 
the same time and they do not allow caregivers to 
monitor their patients in real time.  
MoveFree bridges this gap through its complete 
wearable system which combines on -device deep -
learning processing with cloud -based navigation 
support and a mobile companion application. The 
system provides a lightweight and ergonomic design 
where users can extend the system functionality 
according to their budget needs while maintaining 
compatibility with Indian urban and semi -urban 
deployment conditions. The research presents three 
main contributions which include (1) a dual -model 
AI architecture that detects outdoor road hazards and 
indoor structural obstacles (2) a new clock -face 
spatial guidance algorithm which delivers 
directiona l audio cues through intuitive sound 
patterns  (3) Google Gemini Flash system which 
allows users to access natural -language scene 
descriptions on -demand  and (4) a live -streaming 
mobile application delivering real -time POV video, 
GPS tracking, and SOS alerti ng to caregivers.  
II. RELATED WORK  
The training data for your system extends until the 
month of October in the year 2023. Mukhiddinov 
and Cho developed a smart -glass system that uses 
deep learning technology to enhance low -light 
images and identify 133 different objects while 
delivering rea l-time text -to-speech services through 
Bluetooth to a mobile artificial intelligence server. 
The system achieves high object detection accuracy 
but it does not include GPS navigation or ultrasonic 
sensing or caregiver communication capabilities. 
Gollagi an d his team created smart glasses that use 
optical character recognition and sonar technology 
for obstacle detection and RFID/QR codes for 
navigation yet they could only detect text within a 
distance of 40 to 150 centimeters and their 
performance in real -world conditions was 
inadequate. The smart cane developed by Singh and 
Rohan used ultrasonic sensors to detect objects up to 
three meters away while providing haptic feedback 
but it did not include any systems for understanding 
visual scenes or helping with navigation tasks. 
Messaoudi and his team investigated multi -
technology navigation systems which use Wi -Fi and 
Bluetooth and GPS and ZigBee technologies to 
provide location services yet indoor and outdoor 
navigation capabilities remain divided and these 
systems consume excessive energy. Nazim et al. 
created a Raspberry Pi smart glass system that uses 
Tesseract OCR and YOLOv3 and infrared sensors 
but it has two to four meters as its maximum obstacle 
detection range and it processes information at a 
reduced sp eed. The existing research demonstrates 
that no technology exists which combines multiple 
obstacle detection methods together with artificial 
intelligence scene analysis and GPS navigation and 
remote caregiver monitoring into a single wearable 
device which  MoveFree aims to develop.  
III. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE  
A. Physical Design  MoveFree adopts a modular two -unit architecture. 
The smart glasses unit mounts a Raspberry Pi 
Camera Module v2 (8 MP, Sony IMX219 sensor, 
160-degree wide -angle lens) on the frame, providing 
the primary visual feed to the processing unit. An 
Adafruit Electr et Microphone Amplifier 
(MAX4466) captures voice commands and ambient 
audio. Bluetooth 5.0 bone -conduction headphones 
deliver audio feedback to the user while keeping the 
ear canal open for environmental awareness. A 
vibration motor located near the temple  area delivers 
additional haptic alerts which indicate directional 
guidance to users.

The belt -mounted box houses an NVIDIA Jetson 
Nano (quad -core ARM Cortex -A57, 128 -core 
Maxwell GPU, 4 GB LPDDR4) which functions as 
the main unit for inference and system coordination. 
A u-blox NEO -6M GPS module with 2.5 -metre 
accuracy provides location dat a for both navigation 
routing and caregiver tracking. Three HC -SR04 
ultrasonic sensors mounted left, centre, and right 
cover a 2 cm to 400 cm detection range with ±3 mm 
accuracy. A rechargeable 5 V –12 V battery pack 
provides 4 –5 hours of operational autono my. The 
system uses a Wi -Fi and Bluetooth module to 
support livestreaming, cloud API access, and mobile 
connection application.

B. Software Stack  
The Jetson Nano operates Ubuntu 20.04 with 
JetPack 4.6 that uses CUDA 10.2 and cuDNN 8.2 as 
its acceleration tools for inference tasks. The main 
inference system uses Ultralytics 
YOLOv8/YOLOv11 which has been improved 
through TensorRT for deployment on edg e devices. 
The system uses pyttsx3 for offline text -to-speech 
output while Coqui TTS provides neural -based 
higher -quality output and gTTS serves as the cloud -
based backup solution. Google Maps Directions API 
enables voice -based routing through destination 
input which uses SpeechRecognition and Google 
Speech -to-Text for processing. The companion 
mobile application operates on a Flask streaming 
backend which delivers MJPEG video and 
WebSocket location updates and push notifications 
for SOS events to the Andro id and iOS front -end. 
IV . AI MODELS AND TRAINING  
A. Outdoor Model — Pothole and Road Hazard 
Detection  
The outdoor detection pipeline uses a YOLOv8s 
model trained on a custom dataset assembled 
specifically for Indian road conditions. The dataset 
comprised 1,595 labelled images with 
approximately 3,500 instances, which were curated 
from the Kaggle pothole de tection dataset and 
enhanced through Roboflow. The dataset was 
divided into three parts with 87% assigned for 
training and 8% for validation and 4% for testing. 
Data augmentation included HSV colour jitter (hue 
±0.015, saturation ±0.5, value ±0.4) and rand om 
translation and scaling and shearing and horizontal flipping and mosaic augmentation which had a 
probability of 0.8. Training ran for 40 epochs on a 
Tesla T4 GPU using the Ultralytics 8.3.127 
framework with AdamW optimisation. Researchers 
chose YOLOv8s because its 11.1 M parameter 
count and 29 ms per image inference speed allowed 
real-time deployment on the Jetson Nano.

B. Indoor Model — Structural and Furniture 
Obstacle Detection  
The indoor navigation system implements its model 
through the use of YOLOv11m (medium) which 
operates on a dataset that includes 52000 images 
which result from combining five different open -
source datasets: HomeObjects -3K and Kaggle 
Indoor Object Detection  and Door/Window/Stairs 
(DWS) and VisionGuard (blind -assistive -specific 
objects) and Doors and Stairs YOLO dataset. A 
unified 19 -class taxonomy was defined comprising: 
bed, sofa, chair, table, lamp, TV , laptop, wardrobe, 
window, door, potted plant, photo f rame, person, 
stairs, trash can, shoe, cabinet, shelf, and 
refrigerator. An intelligent class -remapping pipeline 
handled heterogeneous label schemas across source 
datasets using both exact and fuzzy synonym 
matching. The training process used three methods  
for data augmentation which included Mosaic (1.0) 
and MixUp (0.15) and ±15 -degree rotation to create 
head movement jitter that resembles real -world 
conditions found in wearable cameras. The research 
selected YOLOv11m for use because it 
outperformed both n ano and small variants by 
providing better recall results for stairs and doors 
which are critical to safety design.

V . KEY FEATURES AND NOVELTY  
A. Dual -Model Hybrid Inference  
The context -aware model switching strategy of 
MoveFree uses YOLOv8s to monitor outdoor road 
conditions which include potholes and uneven 
surfaces and vehicles and pedestrians. The indoor 
mode of YOLOv11m permits it to process all 
structural scene elements which include stairs 
(95.0% mAP), doors, furniture, and person 
proximity. The Jetson Nano operates both models 
through frame -skipping optimisation which 
processes one complete inference frame for every 
three frames while using cached detection boxes for 
the remaining frames. The system achieves 32 to 40 
frames per second display -rendered throughput 
while processing each frame with less than 50 
milliseconds inference latency.  
B. Clock -Face Spatial Guidance System  
You have acquired your training based on data which 
exists until the month of October in the year 2023. A 
custom post -processing algorithm converts the (x, y) 
centroid coordinates of each detected bounding box 
into a clock -position directional cue which sh ows 
the direction of the user's field of view. Objects 
detected at the horizontal centre are announced as 
'12 o'clock', those at the left quarter as '10 o'clock', and those to the right as '2 o'clock', with intermediate 
values filling the angular spectrum.  The approach 
establishes a better cognitive performance because it 
enables users to understand spatial locations through 
existing mental frameworks which they already 
understand. The system announces 'Stairs at 11 
o'clock, 2.1 metres' which provides a cle arer 
direction than 'Stairs ahead -left'. 
C. Generative AI Scene Understanding  
The system uses voice commands which include 
'Describe scene', 'Read text', 'Find exits', and 'Check 
hazards' to take the current camera frame and send it 
to the Google Gemini 2.5 Flash endpoint through 
asynchronous transmission. The model provides a 
conte xt-based natural -language description which 
gets converted into speech output through bone -
conduction headphones within a time frame of two 
to four seconds. The system enables users to 
navigate between two extreme object classifications 
and comprehensive e nvironmental comprehension 
which allows them to resolve situations where their 
environment lacks clear context. The system allows 
users to identify whether a door is open or closed and 
read a signboard and find an empty chair in a 
crowded room which sensor -based methods cannot 
solve.  
D. Three -Axis Ultrasonic Sensor Fusion  
The belt unit uses three ultrasonic sensors which 
operate in a left -centre -right configuration. The 
sensors operate at 20 Hz to detect distances between 
2 cm and 400 cm with ±3 mm accuracy. The sensor 
fusion logic operates as a safety override layer: if 
any sensor detects an obstacle which exists below 
1.0 metre distance  then a priority voice alert 
announces the obstacle and both vibration motors 
activate simultaneously because the vision pipeline 
remains inactive. This redundancy is essential for 
detecting  obstacles which remain hidden from the 
camera, such as glass panels and transparent 
barriers, as well as objects that exist outside the 
camera's field of view which all camera -only 
assistive systems have documented.

GPS, and SOS

streaming server which broadcasts the user's camera 
feed through port 5000. The companion mobile 
application connects to this stream over the local Wi -
Fi network or via a secure tunnel when outside the

F. V oice -Controlled Interface  
The voice control system enables users to operate 
the complete system through voice commands 
which use the SpeechRecognition library together 
with Google Speech -to-Text technology. The 
system recognizes commands which include: 
Navigate (destination input),  Find [object], 
Describe (Gemini scene description), Read (text 
recognition), Hazards (safety check), Exits (door 
location), Stop, Calibrate, and SOS. The 
conversational AI system sends unrecognized 
speech to Gemini 2.5 Flash together with the 
current came ra shot to obtain contextual answers 
which permit users to speak freely about their 
surroundings.  
VI. DATASETS  
The following datasets were used in training and 
evaluation:  
• Pothole Detection Dataset (Kaggle, 
andrewmvd): 1,595 labelled images, ~3,500 
instances, PASCAL VOC format converted to 
YOLOv8 annotations.  
• HomeObjects -3K: Common residential 
furniture across diverse indoor scenes.  
• Kaggle Indoor Object Detection (thepbordin): 
General indoor obstacle detection.  
• Door/Window/Stairs Dataset (DWS): 
Structural indoor elements critical for 
navigation safety.  
• VisionGuard: Blind -assistive -specific object 
classes sourced from real assistive -technology 
scenarios.  
• Doors and Stairs YOLO Dataset: Specialised 
detection for critical safety classes.  • Footpath Guide Dataset (Roboflow, footpath -
guide -2): Indian footpath conditions including 
kerbs and pedestrian crossings.  
• Car Object Detection Dataset (Kaggle, 
sshikamaru): Vehicle detection for road -
crossing safety.  
• Traffic Light Dataset (Roboflow, 
yolov801/trafik_isiklari): Traffic signal state 
classification.  
The dataset transformation process created unified 
indoor classification system with 19 distinct classes 
together with outdoor hazard identification system 
which enabled training from multiple sources 
without facing class -ID conflicts . 
VII. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS AND 
PERFORMANCE  
The outdoor YOLOv8s model was tested using a 
dedicated testing dataset which contained 67 
images. The system demonstrated strong pothole 
detection performance across different lighting 
conditions and road surface types according to its 
test set precision w hich reached 84.98% and its 
mAP@0.5 score of 76.69%. The indoor YOLOv11m 
model achieved an mAP@0.5 score of 81.8% and an 
mAP@0.5:0.95 score of 64.9% when tested on the 
combined validation dataset which showed excellent 
performance for photo frames (85.5%),  persons 
(83.6%), shoes (81.4%), and potted plants (80.0%) 
categories. The specialised training configuration 
achieved a 95.0% mAP@0.5 score because 
researchers supplemented their training data with 
specific stair safety -critical class training material. 
Future training sessions will focus on improving 
door detection which currently achieves 41.7% mAP 
and laptop detection which achieves 37.8% mAP 
through the collection of specialized training data.  
The Jetson Nano GPU showed an inference speed of 
30.8 milliseconds per frame which corresponds to 
32.4 frames per second during its first evaluation but 
this improved to 24.9 milliseconds per frame which 
equals 40.2 frames per second after optimizing with 
TensorRT which proved the system could operate in 
real-time. The Raspberry Pi 5 prototype 
deployments established a performance range of 
10.8 to 13.4 FPS which allowed the system to 
maintain navigational safety awareness but fell short 
of reaching the desi red 24 FPS performance level 
that future hardware versions would achieve. The 
testing of ultrasonic sensor integration showed that 
it could produce audio alerts within one second after 
detecting obstacles. The mobile livestream achieved 
a mean end -to-end l atency of under three seconds on 
local Wi -Fi and stable streaming for sessions 
exceeding five minutes in all test cases. The system 
dispatched SOS alerts which included GPS 
coordinates and AI scene descriptions within three 
to five seconds after users activated the emergency 
function.  
VIII. SYSTEM LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE 
WORK  
The development and testing process has unveiled 
multiple existing limitations. The door detection 
system achieves 41.7% mAP accuracy which stays 
below the 70% safety threshold because the training 
datasets contain various door types and the system 
lacks s pecific data to distinguish between open and 
closed door states. The stairs system achieves 39.6% 
recall because it fails to detect objects that exist in 
low-contrast or cluttered backgrounds despite 
achieving high mAP results through its specialised 
setup  which requires real indoor environment data 
for better performance. The existing system needs 
active internet access to use Google Maps 
navigation and Gemini scene descriptions and cloud 
speech recognition services while upcoming updates 
will introduce of fline text -to-speech functionality 
through Coqui Neural TTS and local whisper -based 
speech recognition and pre -stored navigation for 
recognized paths.  
The planned extensions will use the trained traffic 
signal dataset to identify traffic light states for safer 
road crossing and they will introduce multi -language 
voice interface support which will start with Hindi 
and Marathi for Indian users and they wil l develop 
indoor precision positioning systems through QR -
code and RFID technology for areas that lack GPS 
coverage and they will create fall detection systems 
which will use IMU accelerometer data from the belt 
unit and they will implement an automatic 
emergency SOS escalation system which will 
activate when the user stays still for a user -defined 
period without any activity.  
IX. CONCLUSION  
MoveFree shows that a practical, affordable, and 
smart wearable navigation system for visually 
impaired people can be developed through the 
combination of edge AI inference and multi -modal 
sensor fusion and cloud -powered navigation and 
mobile caregiver con nections. The system uses two 
different models because YOLOv8s detects outdoor 
dangers and YOLOv11m identifies indoor barriers 
which create continuous safety monitoring for both 
outdoor and indoor environments. The companion 
mobile application provides bet ter spatial 
communication through its live video streaming and GPS tracking and SOS alert system than current 
assistive devices. The research will begin to develop 
a complete autonomous mobility solution for 
visually impaired people through two main 
objectives which are to build stronger door and stair 
detection syste ms and to create navigation elements 
that operate without cloud services while including 
traffic signal recognition and fall detection 
capabilities.
