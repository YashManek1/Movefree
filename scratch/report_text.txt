 Abstract  
 
Navigating public spaces can be a daunting task for individuals who are blind or visually 
impaired, especially in dynamic environments such as busy urban areas, transportation 
systems, and unfamiliar roads. Existing navigation aids often rely on static or limited 
guidance, failing to provide real -time, intelligent, and context -aware assistance.  
MoveFree is an innovative navigation assistant designed to empower visually impaired 
individuals by enhancing their mobility, independence, and safety using real -time 
technologies. It integrates ultrasonic sensors, a YOLOv8[8] computer vision model, Google 
Maps navigation, voice interaction, and live video streaming, all embedded in a wearable 
system comprising smart glasses and a belt -attached processing unit.  
The ultrasonic sensors detect obstacles in close range and issue immediate voice alerts to 
the user, preventing collisions. For advanced object detection like potholes and vehicles, 
MoveFree uses a lightweight YOLOv8 model for real -time visual analysis thr ough the 
onboard camera. In parallel, Google Maps provides spoken turn -by-turn navigation by 
accepting voice -based destination input, allowing hands -free interaction.  
To enhance safety, the system features real -time location sharing and live POV streaming 
to a mobile app used by family members or caregivers. This allows remote monitoring and 
intervention if necessary, creating a secure safety net for the user in crowded  or unfamiliar 
locations.  
The system is designed to be compact, wearable, and responsive —smart glasses include 
the camera and audio output, while the belt unit handles processing, GPS, and power 
supply. The solution also supports future extensions such as traffic light recognition and 
AI-based road crossing assistance.  
In summary, MoveFree offers a smart, scalable, and practical assistive technology solution. 
By combining multi -modal obstacle detection, AI -powered visual processing, and 
seamless communication features, it empowers the visually impaired to move confidentl y 
and safely in complex real -world environments.  
 
Keywords:  Ultrasonic Sensors, Navigation Assistant, Object Detection, Wearable 
Devices, Safety

1. SURVEY  
1.1 Field Survey  
The field survey of MoveFree  was carried out to make our smart glass technology more 
realistic in accordance with the challenges that the visually impaired face. We carried out 
this survey to cover the needs of our product development by gaining insights from more 
than 75 participant s to make sure that we are working with empathy in our product 
development. We tried to identify key challenges in adopting new technology.  
 
Purpose and Intent  
Becoming a bridge between what is possible through technology and what is experience 
for visually impaired people was our overriding intent. Understanding people’s day -to-day 
experiences, from dealing with public spaces to digital information, is crucial f or creating 
MoveFree on a foundation that is rooted in empathy and utility.  
 
Research Objectives  
The research aims to examine three key areas through the survey process. They include 
identification of the most challenging areas, such as orientation and recognition; areas that 
create barriers for users, including cost and usability; and evaluation of d esired features, 
including speech input and the capability to operate offline.  
 
Key findings and insights  
• The data painted a clear direction for developing the device:  
• The biggest problem is navigation, faced by 75% of the respondents while trying to 
move around unfamiliar places.  
• Affordability is one of the main hindrances; 88.3% of the participants responded that 
they were very concerned about high costs, while 64.9% rated the pricing as very 
important.  
• They want reliability, and for the application or the web to be able to work offline, 
which for 75.3% of users is an essential feature.  
• Simple interaction is preferred, as 87% of users chose voice commands and 63.6% 
selected gesture controls as their preferred input methods.

 1.3 Outcome of the Survey  
1. Integration of AI and Deep Learning  
• Integration of AI and Deep Learning is a consistent trend across recent assistive 
technologies. Systems using deep learning (e.g., for object detection and low -light 
enhancement) provide greater contextual understanding of the environment, 
showing potentia l to significantly improve the mobility and safety of visually 
impaired users.  
2. Multimodal Feedback  
• Multimodal Feedback (audio, haptic, tactile) is crucial for usability. Devices that 
combine audio (TTS), haptic (vibration), and tactile (graphics or touch interfaces) 
offer more intuitive and safer user experiences. However, not all systems implement 
these modalities together.  
3. Object Detection and Text Recognition  
• Object Detection and Text Recognition are standard in smart glasses, yet they often 
suffer from:  
• Limited recognition range (typically <1.5 meters).  
• Poor performance under low -light or complex backgrounds.  
• Slow or server -dependent processing (reducing real -time usability).  
4. Obstacle Detection  
• Obstacle Detection using ultrasonic or sonar sensors is common in smart canes and 
hybrid glasses, but such systems:  
• Have limited range and directionality.  
• Require user training.  
• Struggle to detect complex or dynamic obstacles like vehicles or people.  
5. Navigation Support  
• Navigation Support remains fragmented:  
• RFID and QR code -based indoor navigation is promising but requires pre -installed 
infrastructure.  
• GPS is useful for outdoor use but unreliable indoors and in dense environments.  
• Systems using Bluetooth and Wi -Fi for positioning face range and power 
limitations.  
 6. Voice Control and Hands -Free Interaction  
• Voice Control and Hands -Free Interaction are underused. Few systems support 
voice commands for switching modes or interacting with features, which limits 
usability for blind users who cannot easily operate buttons or touchscreens.  
7. Device Comfort, Wearability, and Portability  
• Device Comfort, Wearability, and Portability are often overlooked in technically 
capable devices. Some solutions are heavy, require wired connections, or are not 
ergonomic for long -term use.  
8. Design Thinking and User -Centered Innovation  
• Design Thinking and User -Centered Innovation (e.g., through TRIZ, QFD, opinion 
mining) are beginning to shape future designs. These approaches help align 
technical features with real user needs, but are not yet widely adopted.  
9. Gaps and Opportunities Identified  
• No current system offers an integrated solution with object detection, obstacle 
sensing, traffic signal recognition, navigation, and text reading in one package.  
• Low-light and weather -affected environments remain challenging across most 
systems.  
• There is a lack of multi -language support and real -time, offline processing 
capabilities.  
• Most systems rely on internet connectivity, which may not be available in all user 
environments.  
• Many systems are designed for English -speaking users, ignoring broader linguistic 
accessibility.  
  
 2. NEED OF THE PRODUCT  
 
2.1 Introduction to MoveFree  
MoveFree  is an AI -powered wearable navigation assistant designed to empower visually 
impaired individuals with greater independence and safety during outdoor movement. By 
integrating smart obstacle detection, real -time navigation, and AI -driven assistance into a 
compact wearable system, MoveFree enhances spatial awareness and reduces dependency 
on caregivers.  
The system consists of two main components:  
1. A belt -mounted processing unit that houses sensors (ultrasonic and camera), power 
supply, and the onboard AI model.  
2. Smart glasses equipped with a front -facing camera and audio output, allowing for 
both live perception and real -time feedback.  
MoveFree detects obstacles such as potholes, vehicles, or pedestrians, reads nearby text 
aloud, navigates using GPS, and even streams live video/audio to a caregiver if needed.  
 
Need for the Product  
India alone has over 12 million visually impaired people, many of whom face daily 
challenges navigating uneven roads, unmarked pathways, and fast -moving traffic. 
Traditional tools like white canes or guide dogs provide limited environmental awareness, 
especially in crowded or complex terrains.  
Current commercial solutions either:  
• Lack real -time obstacle classification (e.g., potholes),  
• Are prohibitively expensive (e.g., WeWalk 2.0),  
• Or are not available in Indian markets.  
MoveFree addresses this gap by offering:  
• Affordable, localized obstacle detection (starting with potholes),  
• Real-time auditory guidance,  
• GPS-enabled voice navigation,  
• Edge deployment for portability,  
• A scalable system with AI -enhanced safety features.  
 It aims to bridge the accessibility gap through intelligent, context -aware mobility support, 
making independent travel safer and more achievable for the visually impaired.  
 
2.2 Analysis of Existing systems  
1) Envision  
Overview:  
Envision Glasses are a transformative assistive technology designed to empower people 
who are blind or have low vision by providing seamless, real -time access to visual 
information through spoken descriptions and interactive AI. Rather than simply deliveri ng 
raw data, these glasses translate the visual world into meaningful knowledge, enabling 
users to navigate daily life with greater independence and confidence.  
 
Key Features:  
• Instant Text:  Immediately reads aloud any text that appears in front of you for 
quick access to signs, labels, and menus.  
• Scan Text:  Reads longer documents or multi -page texts out loud in multiple 
languages with accurate layout detection  
• Detect Light:  Identifies the intensity and direction of light sources to aid spatial 
awareness  
• Detect Colo urs: Identifies and names the colors of objects in view.  
• Find People:  Alerts users when people are nearby and can recognize familiar faces 
after being taught  
• Voice Control:  Operate all functions hands -free using voice commands for ease of 
use 
• Multi -language Support:  Reads text in over 60 languages with automatic language 
detection  
 
Disadvantages:  
• Limited Frame Options: Existing users of prescription glasses may find the limited 
choice of frames restrictive, as the device currently offers only a simple, lens -free 
titanium frame or a few designer frames.  
 • Learning Curve and Interface Sensitivity: The touchpad controls can be overly 
sensitive or inconsistent, particularly for two -finger gestures, which may cause 
frustration and require practice to master.  
• Performance Issues in Low Light: The glasses struggle to read text or recognize 
objects accurately in dim lighting conditions, which can reduce their effectiveness 
in many real -world scenarios like restaurants or evening outings  
• Price vs. Benefit:  Some users feel the cost is high relative to the benefits they 
personally receive, emphasizing the importance of trying the device before 
purchasing  
 
Learnings:  
• Better Controls Improve Ease of Use:  Making the device more responsive to 
touch and adding other control options like advanced voice commands can make it 
easier for users to learn and use.  
• Works Well in All Lighting:  Improving how the device performs in low -light or 
very bright places —using better sensors or smarter AI —will help users rely on it 
more throughout the day.           
• Smart Battery Use and Quick Start:  MoveFree should save battery when not in 
use but still be ready to start instantly —audio cues when turning on can make it feel 
smoother and more helpful.           
• Recognizing More Objects Helps Users More:  Adding more types of objects the 
device can recognize, and allowing it to use multiple features (like navigation and 
object detection) at the same time, can give users a richer experience.    
• Affordable and Easy to Try:  Letting people try MoveFree with rental or trial plans 
can help them understand its value before buying, making it more accessible.  
   
2) WeWALK  
Overview:  
WeWALK  Smart Cane 2.0 is an innovative mobility aid designed to significantly enhance 
the independence and safety of people who are visually impaired. It reimagines the 
traditional white cane by integrating intelligent assistance that goes beyond simple obstacle  
detection. This smart cane acts as a reliable companion, providing users with real -time 
information about their environment to help them navigate more confidently and 
efficiently.  
  
Key Features:  
• Enhanced Obstacle Detection: Alerts users to overhead and waist -level obstacles 
through haptic (vibration) and audible feedback, improving safety beyond 
traditional cane capabilities . 
• Intelligent Voice Assistant: Provides voice -activated assistance for queries, 
directions, and travel tips, powered by AI and accessible directly from the cane.  
• Seamless Connectivity: Connects via Bluetooth to smartphones and headphones, 
enabling hands -free operation and integration with navigation apps.     
• Accessible Navigation: Offers turn -by-turn GPS navigation with multimodal 
instructions tailored for blind and low vision users, including clock directions and 
destination tracking   
 
Disadvantages:  
• Weight and Ergonomics:  The cane is often perceived as heavy and bulky, 
especially compared to traditional white canes, causing hand fatigue and discomfort 
during extended use.        
• Slippery Handle Grip:  The handle can become slippery, particularly when the 
user’s hand is sweaty, increasing the risk of dropping the cane.     
• Vibration Feedback Issues:  Continuous vibrations from walking on certain 
surfaces (like sidewalks) can cause hand tingling and make it difficult to distinguish 
obstacle alerts, reducing effectiveness of haptic feedback.    
• Sensitivity and Accidental Commands:  The cane’s controls sometimes trigger 
unintended commands or are overly sensitive, which can frustrate users and 
interrupt navigation.   
• Limited Compatibility with Cane Types:  The device does not attach to all popular 
cane types preferred by users, such as lightweight, rigid canes with metal tips, 
limiting user choice and customization       
• Audio Volume and Clarity:  The built -in speaker’s volume may be insufficient in 
noisy environments, making navigation instructions hard to hear, especially near 
traffic or in crowded areas  
 
Learnings:  
 • Smart Features Increase Independence:  MoveFree , which attaches to regular 
glasses, helps visually impaired people move around more freely and confidently 
by using smart technology.         
• Comfort and Ease of Use Matter:  Even with advanced features like face detection 
and haptic feedback, the device must be lightweight, comfortable, and easy to use 
every day.   
• Works Without Internet When Needed:  Although internet is needed for things 
like Google Maps, important safety features like obstacle detection and vibration 
alerts still work offline, which is important for user trust .   
• Helpful Feedback for Better Awareness:  MoveFree gives clear audio instructions 
and gentle vibrations, helping users understand their surroundings even in noisy or 
busy places.  
• App and Transit Integration Makes Travel Easier:  By connecting with phone 
apps and real -time transit info, MoveFree helps users plan routes and travel more 
easily on their own.

MoveFree  provides real -time voice -guided navigation using GPS and obstacle

By enabling safer and more independent movement, MoveFree facilitates greater

10. Smart City Integration  
MoveFree can integrate with smart city infrastructure for enhanced services like real -
time path updates, rest area detection, or automated emergency routing.  
  
 3. PROBLEM FORMULATION  
 
3.1 Problem Formulation  
MoveFree addresses the challenge of safe, independent navigation for visually impaired 
individuals in dynamic and unstructured environments. It provides real -time obstacle 
detection using ultrasonic sensors and an AI -powered vision system (YOLOv8), 
deliver ing timely voice alerts to avoid collisions. Additionally, the system supports 
voice -command control, point -to-point GPS navigation, and live video + location 
sharing with family members for enhanced monitoring and support. This empowers 
users to move with  confidence in public spaces, reducing dependence on others.  
 
3.2 Product Objectives  
• Real -Time Obstacle Detection: Detect static and dynamic objects (like potholes, 
poles, people, vehicles) using ultrasonic sensors and a YOLOv8 -based object 
detection model, and provide audio alerts.  
• Voice -Based Navigation: Allow users to specify destinations via voice input and 
navigate turn -by-turn using Google Maps, with voice guidance.  
• Live Location & Video Streaming: Enable family members to monitor the user’s 
location and POV video in real time via a connected mobile app for safety and 
assistance.  
• Text Recognition & Read -Aloud (Planned): Use OCR to read text from 
surroundings (signboards, directions) and convert it to speech.  
• Smart Road Crossing (Planned): Detect traffic lights and vehicle movement using 
the front camera and warn the user when unsafe to cross.  
 
3.3 Applications of the Product  
• Urban Navigation: Guides users through crowded streets, detects potholes and 
curbs, and assists in safe road crossings using audio alerts.  
• Public Transport: Helps users locate bus stops, train platforms, and alerts them 
when moving vehicles or platforms are near.  
• Home -to-Destination Navigation: Offers complete voice -controlled point -A to 
point -B GPS navigation with continuous feedback.  
• Safety Monitoring: Real-time streaming of user’s live POV and location to 
caregivers provides peace of mind and emergency support.  
• Educational & Workplace Access: Enables safer travel to schools, colleges, and 
offices, promoting inclusivity and independence.  
 3.4 Novelty  
• Multimodal Obstacle Detection:  Uniquely combines ultrasonic sensors with a 
dual-model computer vision approach —using YOLOv8s[8] for outdoor road 
hazards (potholes, vehicles) and a specialized YOLOv11m model for complex 
indoor environments (stairs, doors, furniture).  
• Clock -Face Guidance System:  Unlike standard "left/right" instructions, 
MoveFree translates spatial data into intuitive angular directions (e.g., "Door at 2 
o'clock, 3 meters"), significantly reducing cognitive load for the user.  
• Generative AI Scene Understanding:  Integrates Google Gemini Flash AI  to 
provide semantic context. Users can ask natural language questions like "Describe 
the room" or "Find the empty chair," and receive detailed, context -aware audio 
descriptions, a feature missing in traditional sensor -based aids.  
• Real -Time Livestreaming:  Streams the user's camera feed and audio to a mobile 
app for family members, providing a direct POV of the environment for remote 
assistance.  
• Voice -Controlled Smart Navigation:  Accepts destination input via voice, 
integrates with Google Maps for outdoor routing, and seamlessly switches context 
for indoor exploration.

 4. PROPOSED DESIGN  
 
The assistive system for the visually impaired is designed around a modular architecture 
divided into two physical units: smart glasses and a belt -mounted processing box. This 
modular division helps in weight distribution, ergonomic wearability, and easier  
component maintenance or upgrades. The smart glasses include input/output sensory 
components, while the processing box houses computational and power -intensive units.  
 
 
Fig 4.1 Proposed System Architecture  
 
Smart Glasses Module  
The glasses are equipped with the following components:  
1. Camera Module (Raspberry Pi Camera v2)  
• Mounted on the frame, this 8MP wide -angle camera (Sony IMX219 sensor) 
captures the user's surroundings in real time.  
• It provides visual data for obstacle detection, traffic light recognition, text 
reading, and livestreaming.  
• The data is fed directly to the Jetson Nano for processing.  
 

  
2. Microphone (Adafruit Electret MAX4466)  
• An omnidirectional microphone captures the user's voice commands and 
ambient sounds.  
• This input is crucial for voice -based navigation and interaction with AI 
assistants.  
3. Bone Conduction Headphones  
• These allow the user to receive audio feedback, such as navigation prompts, 
obstacle alerts, or text -to-speech output, while keeping the ears free to 
maintain environmental awareness.  
• Bluetooth 5.0 ensures wireless, low -latency communication.  
4. Vibration Motor  
• Positioned near the temple, the motor provides haptic feedback (e.g., 
directional alerts, warnings for close objects) to enhance non -verbal 
awareness.  
 
Belt-Mounted Box Module  
The belt -mounted enclosure houses the processing and communication components. It 
includes:  
1. Jetson Nano (Core Processing Unit) [10] 
• A powerful AI edge computing platform, the Jetson Nano supports real -time 
inference using models optimized with PyTorch and TensorRT.  
• It runs a hybrid architecture: a lightweight YOLOv8s[8] model for outdoor 
hazard detection and a robust YOLOv11m[8] model for detailed indoor 
object recognition.  
• It processes inputs from the camera and sensors to execute the Clock -Face 
Guidance Algorithm, translating bounding box coordinates into relative 
clock positions for the user.  
2. Power Supply  
• A rechargeable 5V –12V battery pack powers the system with an estimated 
backup of 4 –5 hours based on the processing load.  
3. Cooling System  
• A small fan or passive heatsink is added to prevent thermal throttling during 
heavy computation.  
4. Storage (SD Card or External SSD)  
• Storage is used for logging data, storing AI models, and managing offline 
TTS software like Coqui TTS or pyttsx3.  
 5. Wi-Fi + Bluetooth Module  
• Enables livestreaming, cloud sync, and low -latency communication with the 
Google Gemini API for generating real -time scene descriptions (e.g., reading 
text, identifying surroundings).  
6. Ultrasonic Sensors (3 Units - Left, Center, Right)  
• Each sensor has a range of 2 cm to 400 cm with ±3 mm accuracy.  
• These sensors help in detecting obstacles and determining safe paths through 
triangulated input.  
7. u-blox NEO -6M GPS Module  
• This compact GPS module with 2.5 m accuracy is used for point -to-point 
navigation and location sharing with family members.  
• It connects via UART and supports external antennas for better reception.  
Interconnectivity and Functionality  
The entire system is controlled by the Jetson Nano, which acts as the brain of the project. 
Sensor inputs from the ultrasonic modules and the GPS module, along with image and 
audio inputs from the camera and microphone, are processed to perform AI -based de cision -
making. Outputs are provided via speech (text -to-speech), livestream (via Wi -Fi), and 
haptic or audio feedback (via headphones and motors). All components are connected via

Model Implementation in MoveFree  
5.1. Outdoor Model Implementation  
1. Objective  
To accurately detect obstacles (starting with potholes) in real -time for visually impaired 
users, using a lightweight and efficient object detection model deployed on portable 
hardware.  
2. Model Selection  
• Model Used: YOLOv8s (Ultralytics)  
• Why YOLOv8s?  
o Lightweight architecture (11.1M parameters)  
o Fast inference (29.05 ms/image ≈ 34 FPS)  
o Ideal for real -time edge deployment (Jetson Nano, Raspberry Pi + Coral, 
etc.) 
o Robust performance on small custom datasets  
3. Dataset Details  
• Type: Custom -labeled dataset focused on detecting potholes  
• Total Images: 1,595  
• Total Instances: ~3,500  
• Labeling Tool: Roboflow  
• Split:  
o Training: 1,395 images (87%)  
o Validation: 133 images (8%)  
o Testing: 67 images (4%)  
• Format: YOLOv8 -compatible (image + .txt annotations)  
4. Data Augmentation  
Applied using Roboflow and Ultralytics pipeline:  
• Color Augmentations:  
o HSV: h=0.015, s=0.5, v=0.4  
• Geometric Transformations:  
 o translate=0.1, scale=0.3, shear=0.01, degrees=0.1  
• Others:  
o perspective=0.0002, fliplr=0.5  
o mosaic=0.8, auto_augment='randaugment'  
5. Training Details  
• Epochs: 40  
• Hardware: Tesla T4 GPU (Colab Pro)  
• Framework: Ultralytics YOLOv8.3.127  
• Caching: Enabled for faster preprocessing

5.2. Indoor Model Implementation (Obstacle & Hazard Detection)  
1. Objective  
To enable safe indoor navigation by detecting structural elements (stairs, doors) and 
furniture with high precision, while providing semantic understanding of the 
environment.  

 2. Model Selection  
• Model Used:  YOLOv11m (Medium)  
• Why YOLOv11m?  
o Superior accuracy for detecting complex indoor classes compared to 
nano/small models.  
o Optimized for identifying safety -critical hazards like "Stairs" and "Doors" 
which require high recall.  
o Fine-tuned to handle varying indoor lighting conditions and occlusion.  
3. Dataset Details  
• Composition:  A massive composite dataset of ~52,000 images  was curated by 
merging five open -source datasets:  
o HomeObjects -3K (Common furniture)  
o Kaggle Indoor Object Detection  (General obstacles)  
o Door/Window/Stairs Dataset  (Structural elements)  
o VisionGuard  (Blind -assistive specific objects)  
o Doors & Stairs YOLO  (Specialized detection)  
• Classes (19 Total):  Bed, Sofa, Chair, Table, Lamp, TV, Laptop, Wardrobe, 
Window, Door, Potted Plant, Photo Frame, Person, Stairs, Trash Can, Shoe, 
Cabinet, Shelf, Refrigerator.  
4. Training & Optimization  
• Augmentation:  Utilized Mosaic augmentation (1.0), MixUp (0.15), and rotation 
(+/- 15 degrees) to simulate the natural head movement and camera jitter associated 
with wearable devices.  
• Performance:  The indoor model achieved exceptional reliability on safety -critical 
classes:  
o Stairs:  95.0% mAP (Critical for fall prevention)  
o Person:  95.6% mAP  
o Shoe:  96.5% mAP (Trip hazard detection)  
5. Advanced Features Implementation  
• Clock -Face Guidance:  A custom algorithm maps the center -point ($x, y$) of 
detected bounding boxes to a clock sector (1 –12) relative to the user's field of view.  
• Generative AI Integration:  The system captures frames on voice command 
("Describe scene") and transmits them to the Google Gemini Flash  endpoint to 
generate descriptive audio summaries, bridging the gap between simple object 
detection and full scene understanding.
